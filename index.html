About This Course

So, you want to write better? (Or, let's be honest, at least complete your AUCC composition requirement?) In CO150, you'll improve your writing skills, but that means more than learning where to put commas. Writing in CO150 means communicating effectively with your audience—whether that's a research paper for an academic audience, a conversation with peers, or an email to a potential employer. In other words, writing in CO150 is about rhetoric.

WHAT DO WE DO IN CO150?

CO150 may be the smallest class you take at CSU. That's intentional. Foremost, our class is a community. We will encourage each other, give helpful feedback, learn from one another, and engage with multiple viewpoints on a variety of issues that are important to the broader community of a healthy, critical, and engaged University body. With that, your presence (body and mind!) in our class is valuable—not just for your own learning outcomes, but for the benefit of the whole class. You are an individual whose opinions and life experiences offer all of us enhanced understandings of our curriculum.

As you can see from the graphic to the right, projects take up the majority of the "graded" material in class; with that, we approach writing as a process. Our in-class discussions, homework, lectures, group work, and writing all build skills you need to accomplish the larger writing projects.

Projects

P1: Rhetorical Analysis

We'll learn rhetorical terminology and critical reading skills to evaluate a text's effectiveness with its audience in a rhetorical analysis essay to determine how/if the writer achieved their purpose with their audience.

P2: Stakeholder Proposal

Apply the rhetorical skills you learned about while evaluating your text for P1. In P2, you'll write a proposal for an audience who has a stake in a particular issue or problem. This requires adapting your writing to the genre of a written proposal.

P3: Researched Argument

Project three comprises two parts: an annotated bibliography of sources, and a researched argument paper that uses those sources, plus your audience appeals from P2, to join a converstation by writing a researched argument over a debatable topic.

P4: Transfer

In Project 4, you'll transfer all your research, evaluation, and rhetorical skills into crafting a resume and cover letter to "apply" for a job. You'll then evaluate and explain your own rhetorical choices in a reflection essay.
