Projects

Major Projects/Assignment Sheets

Throughout the semester, you'll have 4 major projects that count for 85% of your grade. Each project builds on the rhetorical principles we'll learn along the way. For each project, you'll focus on a specific purpose and develop your ideas for a particular audience.You'll write for a variety of genres which will require various ways of organizing your ideas. Finally, you'll ensure that the style and conventions are not only genre-appropriate, but also meet your purpose for your audience.


The above describes the way we approach the writing process in CO150 by considering the hierarchy of rhetorical concerns. Your projects are evaluated according to how well you address these rhetorical concerns, with the most weight given to purpose & focus, then development for your audience, and so on. Each project assignment sheet explains the rhetorical hierarchy for that particular project.

The rhetorical hierarchy of concerns is less a rubric, and more a visualization of a particular way of thinking about writing that we'll use in CO150. You'll hear plenty more about this in class.

Intro

Specifies due dates / workshop dates, etc.

Purpose and Focus

What's the purpose of this assignment? What is the particular focus? What are you trying to achieve with this project?

Development for Audience

Who are you talking to in this piece? What do they value? How should you appeal to them specifically? How will you use audience appeals, provide evidence to back up your points for this particular person or group, and what do they need to know about this topic?

Genre and Organization

What are the genre conventions that your audience will expect? How does the genre determine how your organize your ideas?

Style and Conventions

How do your grammar, style, and mechanics work for your audience and for the specific genre?

Projects

P1: Rhetorical Analysis

We'll learn rhetorical terminology and critical reading skills to evaluate a text's effectiveness with its audience in a rhetorical analysis essay to determine how/if the writer achieved their purpose with their audience.

P2: Stakeholder Proposal

Apply the rhetorical skills you learned about while evaluating your text for P1. In P2, you'll write a proposal for an audience who has a stake in a particular issue or problem. This requires adapting your writing to the genre of a written proposal.

P3: Researched Argument

Project three comprises two parts: an annotated bibliography of sources, and a researched argument paper that uses those sources, plus your audience appeals from P2, to join a converstation by writing a researched argument over a debatable topic.

P4: Transfer

In Project 4, you'll transfer all your research, evaluation, and rhetorical skills into crafting a resume and cover letter to "apply" for a job. You'll then evaluate and explain your own rhetorical choices in a reflection essay.
