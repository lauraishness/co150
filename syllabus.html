1.0 Required Materials

  • Ethics in Higher Education: A Reader for Writers (referred to as EHE)
  • Joining the Conversation, Second Edition by Mike Palmquist and Barbara Wallraff
  • CO150 Canvas Page - canvas.colostate.edu

  • Notebook paper or spiral journal and pen/pencil for classwork

1.1 Course Description and Objectives

CO150’s aim, in addition to improving writing, research, and critical thinking skills, is to prepare students to write effectively in differing contexts, across disciplines, and for various audiences—both within the university and in any of the increasingly communication-dependent environments (both personally and professionally) that students find themselves in today.

Writing effectively means becoming a critical consumer and producer of information through active reading/listening, thoughtful inquiry, diligent research, and informed contribution to a conversation. CO150 frames writing in terms of joining a conversation, a metaphor for learning to “listen” before speaking or writing, and then contributing something meaningful and relevant to the discussion. By considering the demands of the rhetorical situation and broader conversation, students will improve the purpose, focus, audience appeal, organization, and style and mechanics of their writing.

The focus of CO150 is rhetoric. While the word is worthy of complex, book-length definitions, the core understanding of rhetoric involves comprehension of what words DO in addition to what they state. Throughout the semester, students will build skills to both recognize and use the rhetorical strategies for persuasion and argumentation.

To ground our study of rhetoric, we will read, write, and discuss topics and issues related to our course theme, Ethics in Higher Education. As you learn to sift through ideas, evidence, claims, assumptions, values, and opinions, you will be asked to consider how something is said as much as what is said. We have done our best to include a representative range of the voices in the readings for the class.  By studying a variety of writers, we will analyze how authors craft texts for particular audiences and purposes.

Key Objectives:

  • Developing critical reading practices to support research and writing;
  • Understanding writing as a rhetorical practice, ie choosing effective strategies for addressing purpose, audience, and contexts;
  • Learning important elements of academic discourse, such as posing and critically investigating questions, using sources effectively and ethically, and writing effective summaries, analyses, and arguments;
  • Increasing information literacy through strategies for locating, selecting, and evaluating sources for inquiry;
  • Developing effective research and writing processes, including peer collaboration and response and using feedback to guide revision.

TL;DR: you'll learn how to write better for an audience, with an actual purpose rather than willy-nilly; you'll also learn critical thinking, research skills, probably lots of other stuff.

2.0 Grades

Projects will be assessed using a grading guide on the project assignment sheet. Generally, however, each essay is assessed using the hierarchy of rhetorical concerns. (See assignment sheets and Section 8 for helpful diagrams.)

It is expected that all assignments will be carefully prepared and proofread.  Unless otherwise noted, all assignments will be typed using 12-point, Times or Arial font, double-spaced, 1-inch margins, numbered pages, and nicely stapled. MLA format and guidelines must be used for all applicable assignments.

Project deadlines mean that papers must be submitted to Canvas or sent to me by email by the designated time (on the assignment sheet or canvas) on the due date to receive full credit.

TL;DR: Your 4 major projects are worth 85% of the your grade, other work is worth 15%. No late work accepted without grading penalty. No revisions accepted.

3.0 Attendance / Tardies Policy

Attendance and participation are not only vital for your success, they’re vital for the success of the class as a whole. This course is constructed with the idea that writing is a process; the work we do in class contributes significantly to the process of writing that culminates in each unit with the major projects. Missing class forfeits your opportunity to add to discussions and help spread out the writing process in ways that make writing major projects more more efficient and successful.

How I take attendance / make up work
  • Starting on the third day of class, I will take attendance every day by handing around a sign-in sheet. It is your responsibility to sign the attendance sheet for class each day since this is the proof of your attendance. If you have not signed the attendance sheet, that means you were not in class.
  • If you are absent, you cannot make up any in-class work we did that day (e.g. group work, discussion, quizzes, in-class writing activities, workshop, etc.).

Please communicate with me if there are any extenuating circumstances that may affect your attendance.

Tardies

Be on time and prepared for class each day. Entering late harms our classroom community by interrupting class, distracting your peers and instructor from the learning process, and causing you to miss important information. If you are late to class and miss handing in homework, a quiz, in-class writing, or similar activity, you cannot make it up. Also, please do not leave early or start packing up your notebooks and backpacks before class is over.

TL;DR: Showing up to class (and on time) is in your best interest.

4.0 Assignments / Due Dates

You are responsible for knowing what assignments are due, when they are due, what assignments you may have missed, and what in-class handouts you need to obtain. Due dates for major projects are at the top of your assignment sheets, on this syllabus, and posted on Canvas; due dates for process work/homework are posted Canvas, usually on a day-by-day basis.

Due dates are not “optional,” nor are they “suggestions.” They are real; respect them. All assignments are due at the beginning of class—most must be uploaded to Canvas or emailed directly to me prior to the beginning of class, unless otherwise stated. Problems with technology are no excuse for missing a due date; you should buffer in time for computer malfunctions such as poor internet connection or an inkless printer. Also, save your work in multiple places to alleviate the stress of computer failure.

I do not accept late work for full credit. However, see TIME MACHINE Section for your optional one extension on a major project.

Absence does not excuse you from turning in an assignment. If forced to miss a class on a due date (for an excused absence or unexcused), that assignment must be turned in ahead of time to receive credit.

TL;DR: Turn in your stuff on time. I don't take late work.

4.1 Time Machine

You may turn in ONE major project (or, in the case of Project 3, one part of that project) a single class period late (or its equivalent). For instance, if the paper is due on a Friday, you may turn it on a Monday.

Rules for using your time machine option:
  • You must inform me in advance by email that you are using your time machine. If you choose to turn in a paper late, you must contact me prior to the paper being due.
  • You may only use your time machine with Projects 1, 2, or 3. You cannot use your time machine with Project 4, nor can you use it for quizzes, workshop drafts, homework assignments, discussions, or any other participation options.

TL;DR: You get one extension on a major project. Use it wisely and let me know about it before the due date.

5.0 Classroom Expectations

Please respect your peers and your instructor through your civility in class. It is quite likely that we will broach sensitive, charged, controversial topics. Therefore, it is absolutely necessary that civility and respect be employed at all times. Respect the opinions of other students and the authors we read (even if you don’t agree). I reserve the right to dismiss any student who disrupts class. Please bring any concerns to my attention immediately.

Please hold each other accountable for the use of technology in the classroom. Cell phones and other devices might prove useful for coursework, but they can also distract you and others. Please be considerate and silence your ringer and refrain from texting in class.

I do not mind if you politely excuse yourself should you have a communication need in the case of an emergency or any pressing academic, family, or professional matter.

TL;DR: Be a respectful human adult, not a meany-pants who's rude to folks or a cyborg attached to your phone.

5.1 Academic Honesty

Plagiarism will not be tolerated in this class. Any instance of plagiarism will result in a reduced or failing grade of the assignment, failure of the course, and/or removal of the Repeat/Delete option for the course. Ignorance is not an excuse for “accidental” plagiarism.

The following are examples of plagiarism (though this list is not exhaustive):
  • Submitting someone else’s work as your own;
  • Submitting a paper that was purchased or downloaded from the internet;
  • Using someone else’s ideas, language, or structure and not properly attributing them;
  • Using a phrase (whether quotation or paraphrase) without acknowledging its source;
  • Using a source without citing it correctly;
  • “Padding” a bibliography by making up sources or citing a source you didn’t use in your research.

To encourage academic honesty in all assignments, each major assignment will require that you include an Honor Pledge indicating that you have not given, received, or used any unauthorized assistance. On each major project, you will write the following honor pledge:

“I have not given, received, or used any unauthorized assistance.”

This honor pledge supersedes any requirement to submit any work to an online plagiarism detection database; I do not require that you use these services. I encourage an atmosphere of mutual respect for the intellectual property of your work, your peers’ work, and the work of other authors whom you attribute in your research. The accountability for your work is based on my assessment and your integrity, not a for-profit software service that may or may not accurately detect legitimate cases of plagiarism.

This class adheres to the Academic Integrity Policy of the General Catalog and the Student Code of Conduct. Please see these documents for further details. In cases of suspected academic dishonesty, all instructors at CSU are required to forward documentary evidence to the Office of Conflict Resolution and Student Conduct Services. This office will ensure due process and advise instructors regarding appropriate disposition of the case.

TL;DR: Don't cheat on anything and risk that I was born yesterday. (SPOILER ALERT! I wasn't.) I don't use sites like turnitin.com for ranty reasons that I'm happy to rant about sometime.

5.2 Expectation of Workload

In addition to the glorious 150 minutes we spend together in class each week, you will be expected to be able to spend a minimum of six hours outside of class time per week on this course. This will include reading for the course, preparing for class, writing discussion posts, drafting papers, etc. Please plan your schedule accordingly so you can devote sufficient time to our class and, consequently, get the most from the course.

TL;DR: You'll have stuff to read and do outside of the time we spend together each week.

5.3 Online Discussion Posts

Part of your participation/process grade in this class will be forum posts which we'll use occasionally (see calendar for specific due dates.) During the weeks we have a forum prompt, unless otherwise noted, you must respond to the forum post by the due date by 5pm with a thorough, thoughtful response of at least 250 words (unless noted otherwise on the particular forum itself.)

This, like all other due dates in this class, is firm. Any responses posted after the deadline will be considered late and will receive no points.

TL;DR: You'll have some online canvas discussions.

5.4 Workshop Policy

Peer collaboration is vital to the writing process, since it’s through collaborating with others that we see and understand things we might not have otherwise. As such I schedule time to workshop every major writing assignment you will do this semester.

You must bring an 80% draft to every workshop. This doesn’t mean a “final” or a “perfect” draft – it means having a draft that is at least 80% of the minimum word count for the project so that you can get adequate feedback on your ideas from your peers. (For example, if the final draft of an assignment must be minimum 1200 words, the workshop draft must be minimum 960 words.)

I grade workshop participation using two criteria:
  1. Submitting your draft on Canvas ahead of time for length
  2. Attending and actively engaging with the workshop

TL;DR: You'll have a workshop before each major project. Submit your draft online. Come to class and give effective feedback to your peers.

6.2 Title IX Resources

CSU’s Student Sexual Harassment and Violence policy, following national guidance from the Office of Civil Rights, requires that faculty follow CSU policy as a “mandatory reporter” of any personal disclosure of sexual harassment, abuse, and/or violence related experiences or incidents shared with the faculty member in person, via email, and/or in classroom papers or homework exercises. These disclosures include but are not limited to reports of personal relational abuse, relational/domestic violence, and stalking. While faculty are often able to help students locate appropriate channels of assistance on campus (e.g., see the CSU Health Network link below), disclosure by the student to the faculty member requires that the faculty member inform appropriate CSU channels to help ensure that the student’s safety and welfare is being addressed, even if the student requests that the disclosure not be shared. 

For counseling support and assistance, please see the CSU Health Network, which includes a variety of counseling services that can be accessed at: health.colostate.edu

And, the Sexual Assault Victim Assistance Team is a confidential student resource that does not have a reporting requirement and that can be of great help to students who have experienced sexual assault. The web address is wgac.colostate.edu/need-help-support.

6.4 Early Academic Reporting

This is a campus-wide program that CASA and Student Affairs operates. Although we begin each semester with great intentions, sometimes we find ourselves a bit off-course or unable to really gain traction so early in the semester (and who can blame you! So many big changes all at once). That is why I, as your instructor who cares, participate in a campus-wide program called “Early Performance Feedback.” Early Performance Feedback is a way for you to find your way back on course if you’ve drifted.

Here’s how it works:

After the fifth week of classes I send an “S” (satisfactory performance) or “U” (unsatisfactory performance) indication to the folks who facilitate Early Performance (the Division of Student Affairs and CASA primarily).

If you receive a “U” indicator, it means that you would benefit from some individualized conversation about resources available to you and a plan of action to set you on the right path. CASA and Student Affairs will send you information about these resources and an invitation for this individualized plan of action. It is NOT any kind of official grade—it is merely a performance indicator.

TL;DR: CSU offers many resources to you as a student.

7.1 Agreement

CO150 is not an easy class, nor should it be. The skills you learn in this class will prepare you for the critical reading, writing, and thinking skills you will need in your future classes and writing outside of the university context. It is meant to push you intellectually and will require significant effort on your part.

Your decision to remain in this section of CO150 demonstrates your agreement to the policies stated above and also demonstrates your willingness to make a commitment to the class and your writing.

As your instructor, I also make a commitment to this class and my students, and am here to help you with your writing and critical thinking. I will help you understand how your writing and thinking can improve and then provide suggestions. If you cannot meet me during my office hours, please contact me to make an appointment.

TL;DR: CO150 can be difficult, but I'm committed to helping you succeed.
