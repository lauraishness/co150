1.0 Required Materials

  • Ethics in Higher Education: A Reader for Writers (referred to as EHE)
  • Joining the Conversation, Second Edition by Mike Palmquist and Barbara Wallraff
  • CO150 Canvas Page - canvas.colostate.edu
  • CO150 Course Website - co150.com
  • Access to reliable means of printing, word processing, and internet connection
  • Notebook paper or spiral journal and pen/pencil for classwork

1.1 Course Description and Objectives

CO150’s aim, in addition to improving writing, research, and critical thinking skills, is to prepare students to write effectively in differing contexts, across disciplines, and for various audiences—both within the university and in any of the increasingly communication-dependent environments (both personally and professionally) that students find themselves in today.

Writing effectively means becoming a critical consumer and producer of information through active reading/listening, thoughtful inquiry, diligent research, and informed contribution to a conversation. CO150 frames writing in terms of joining a conversation, a metaphor for learning to “listen” before speaking or writing, and then contributing something meaningful and relevant to the discussion. By considering the demands of the rhetorical situation and broader conversation, students will improve the purpose, focus, audience appeal, organization, and style and mechanics of their writing.

The focus of CO150 is rhetoric. While the word is worthy of complex, book-length definitions, the core understanding of rhetoric involves comprehension of what words DO in addition to what they state. Throughout the semester, students will build skills to both recognize and use the rhetorical strategies for persuasion and argumentation.

To ground our study of rhetoric, we will read, write, and discuss topics and issues related to our course theme, Ethics in Higher Education. As you learn to sift through ideas, evidence, claims, assumptions, values, and opinions, you will be asked to consider how something is said as much as what is said. We have done our best to include a representative range of the voices in the readings for the class.  By studying a variety of writers, we will analyze how authors craft texts for particular audiences and purposes.

Key Objectives:

  • Developing critical reading practices to support research and writing;
  • Understanding writing as a rhetorical practice, ie choosing effective strategies for addressing purpose, audience, and contexts;
  • Learning important elements of academic discourse, such as posing and critically investigating questions, using sources effectively and ethically, and writing effective summaries, analyses, and arguments;
  • Increasing information literacy through strategies for locating, selecting, and evaluating sources for inquiry;
  • Developing effective research and writing processes, including peer collaboration and response and using feedback to guide revision.

2.0 Grades

Projects will be assessed using a grading guide specific to the assignment. This guide will be distributed with each project assignment sheet. Generally, however, each essay is assessed using the hierarchy of rhetorical concerns. (See assignment sheets and Section 8 for helpful diagrams.) It is expected that all assignments will be carefully prepared and proofread.  Unless otherwise noted, all assignments will be typed using 12-point, Times or Arial font, double-spaced, 1-inch margins, numbered pages, and nicely stapled. MLA format and guidelines must be used for all applicable assignments.

Project deadlines mean that papers must be submitted to Canvas or sent to me by email by the designated time (on the assignment sheet or canvas) on the due date to receive full credit.

P1: Rhetorical Analysis

We'll learn rhetorical terminology and critical reading skills to evaluate a text's effectiveness with its audience in a rhetorical analysis essay to determine how/if the writer achieved their purpose with their audience.

P2: Stakeholder Proposal

Apply the rhetorical skills you learned about while evaluating your text for P1. In P2, you'll write a proposal for an audience who has a stake in a particular issue or problem. This requires adapting your writing to the genre of a written proposal.

P3: Researched Argument

Project three comprises two parts: an annotated bibliography of sources, and a researched argument paper that uses those sources, plus your audience appeals from P2, to join a converstation by writing a researched argument over a debatable topic.

P4: Transfer

In Project 4, you'll transfer all your research, evaluation, and rhetorical skills into crafting a resume and cover letter to "apply" for a job. You'll then evaluate and explain your own rhetorical choices in a reflection essay.
